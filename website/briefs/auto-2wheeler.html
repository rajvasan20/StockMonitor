Two-Wheelers FY2025 10 min read

The EV bet that divides India's motorcycle kings

3 annual reports. 3 radically different strategies. One question: how fast does the EV transition actually happen?

Bajaj Auto · Rs 50,010 Cr revenue · 20.2% EBITDA Hero MotoCorp · Rs 40,756 Cr revenue · 14.4% EBITDA Eicher Motors · Rs 18,451 Cr revenue · 25.8% EBITDA
Context

The Setup

Indian two-wheelers grew 9.1% in FY2025 to 19.6 million units — still below the pre-pandemic peak. Motorcycles (12.3M units) grew a modest 5.1%, while electric 2Ws (1.2M units) surged 20–22% to reach 6.7% market share. Exports recovered strongly at 21%. Three companies, three radically different margin profiles — Eicher at 25.8%, Bajaj at 20.2%, Hero at 14.4% — and a single question that divides them.

Priced In

The Consensus

All three managements converge on these themes — meaning the market already knows:

  1. India macro is supportive. 6.5% GDP, RBI rate cuts, Budget 2025 tax relief. Personal mobility demand grows with the economy. Table stakes.
  2. Rural demand is recovering. Rural outperformed urban in FY2025 (Hero). Good monsoon, better farm incomes. Expected.
  3. Premiumisation is secular. Rising incomes push buyers from 100cc to 125cc+ to 250cc+. All three investing in premium. Not new.
  4. Exports are back. Double-digit export growth after FY23-24 headwinds. LATAM, ASEAN, Africa. Recovery was anticipated.
  5. EV is inevitable. All three acknowledge the transition. But — and this is where consensus shatters — they disagree violently on timing and commitment.
Alpha

The Divergence

Read these three annual reports together, and the strategic fault lines are stark. These companies are making fundamentally different bets on the future of mobility.

Bajaj's KTM acquisition is the highest-stakes bet in the sector. The €800M debt package to acquire the parent of a restructuring European motorcycle brand is either a masterstroke (global premium access at distressed prices) or a value trap (Rs 915 Cr loss already booked). No other Indian 2W company is making a bet this large or this binary.

4. Leadership Stability

Both Bajaj and Hero had disruptions in FY2025:

  • Hero: CEO Niranjan Gupta departed. Operating under an "Acting CEO" arrangement. For the world's largest 2W manufacturer, this is non-trivial.
  • Bajaj: No CEO disruption, but KTM restructuring adds board-level complexity.
  • Eicher: Stable leadership. Siddhartha Lal continues as MD. Most continuity among the three.

The Edge

The Signal

5 things only visible when you read all 3 annual reports together:

1. Bajaj is becoming an EV company faster than the market realizes

Rs 5,500 Cr in EV revenue, 20% of domestic sales, #1 e-scooter, #1 e-3W. Add captive financing (BACL at Rs 9,300 Cr AUM in year one) and the CNG bike (Freedom — world's first). Bajaj is building optionality across every powertrain. The risk: spreading too thin. Motorcycle market share dropped from 18.2% to 16.6% while chasing new frontiers.

2. Eicher is making the contrarian ICE bet — and the math supports it

At 25.8% EBITDA and 87% midsize share, Royal Enfield's ICE business generates Rs 4,279 Cr PAT on just 10 lakh units. Flying Flea is insurance, not strategy. Eicher is explicitly saying: "The EV transition in motorcycles will take longer than scooters." If they're right, they're the most capital-efficient franchise in Indian auto. If wrong, they're the Kodak of 2Ws.

3. Hero's margin problem is existential, not cyclical

14.4% EBITDA on 59 lakh units — the lowest margin on the highest volume. The entry segment (100–110cc) is commoditizing. Hero's premiumisation push puts it directly against Bajaj and Eicher in segments where they're established. The CEO departure mid-year adds execution risk. Hero needs to prove it can move upmarket and maintain volume simultaneously — the hardest trick in the business.

4. Balance sheets are uniformly fortress-strong

All three companies are debt-free with Rs 7,000–17,000 Cr in surplus cash. This means every company can self-fund its EV transition, international expansion, and M&A without dilution. The capital allocation decisions — not the availability of capital — will determine the winners.

5. Captive financing is the emerging competitive lever

Bajaj launched BACL and hit Rs 9,300 Cr AUM in year one. Hero has 65% finance penetration. Eicher at 61%. In rural/semi-urban India, access to credit determines purchase decisions. The company that controls the financing stack controls the customer. Bajaj building its own NBFC while Hero relies on third-party financiers is a structural advantage that compounds over time.
